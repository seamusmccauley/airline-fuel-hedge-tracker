Why your summer 2027 flight could cost more — and what to do about it now

UK airlines are shielded from today's high fuel prices — for now. Our interactive tracker shows you when that protection runs out, and what it means for when to book.

26 March 2026 · 7 min read · Holiday Extras editorial team

What is fuel hedging, and why should you care about it right now?

Right now, most UK airlines are spending less on fuel than you might expect. Not because prices have dropped — they haven't. Jet fuel costs more in 2026 than at almost any point in recent history, driven by geopolitical pressures and supply uncertainty.

The reason airlines aren't feeling it yet is something called fuel hedging. Airlines buy contracts months or even years in advance that lock in a fixed price for the fuel they'll need later. If prices rise after that, the airline is protected — they already bought their fuel at the lower price.

Most major UK carriers did exactly this in 2023 and 2024, when fuel was cheaper. Their 2026 flying costs are largely shielded from today's higher prices. That protection gets passed on to you — flights this year are cheaper than they'd otherwise be.

The problem is those contracts are running out.

As each airline's hedged fuel gets used up and they have to buy at today's prices, fares will rise. Some airlines are better protected than others. Some are exposed as early as late 2026. Others may hold through most of 2027 before the impact hits.
